How is the range of an electric vehicle calculated?

Calculation modes

Quick estimation mode using simplified factors for everyday use. This mode is primarily informational and provides approximate results that may differ from real-world conditions.

Calculation formula:

Range = BaseRange × Battery 100 × Season × Road × Mode × Weight × Climate

Factors applied:

  • Battery Level — percentage of available energy
  • Season — efficiency coefficient (summer/autumn/winter/spring)
  • Road Conditions — route type (city/highway/mixed)
  • Driving Mode — power profile (eco/normal/sport)
  • Weight Load — mass impact on energy consumption
  • Climate Control — HVAC system usage penalty

Note: This mode provides a general estimate and is best suited for quick planning rather than precise range prediction.

Advanced calculation mode with detailed environmental and vehicle parameters for more accurate range prediction that better reflects real-world driving conditions.

Additional factors beyond Standard mode:

  • Speed — aerodynamic drag increases exponentially with velocity
  • Weather — temperature, wind direction/speed, and precipitation
  • Road Relief — elevation changes and terrain profile
  • Wheels & Tires — size, pressure, and rolling resistance
  • Battery Degradation — reduced capacity from aging
  • Energy Consumers — seat/wheel heating, defrost, multimedia

Extended mode combines multiple physics-based models to simulate energy consumption more accurately across various driving scenarios.

Factors affecting mileage

The available energy in the battery is the base of the final range. Lower charge means fewer available kilometers.
Season changes average efficiency because of ambient temperature and typical driving conditions.
City, highway and mixed routes have different efficiency profiles due to speed patterns and aerodynamic losses.
Eco, Normal and Sport change power demand and response, which directly affects energy consumption.
More vehicle mass requires more energy for acceleration and climbs, reducing range.
Cabin heating and cooling consume extra power and can noticeably lower range, especially in extreme weather.
Main factors
The available energy in the battery is the base of the final range. Lower charge means fewer available kilometers.
Eco, Normal and Sport change power demand and response, which directly affects energy consumption.
More vehicle mass requires more energy for acceleration and climbs, reducing range.
Higher speed increases aerodynamic drag, so energy use rises quickly at fast cruising speeds.
Weather combines temperature, wind and precipitation effects into a major real-world efficiency factor.
Hilly and mountain routes require more energy for climbing, which lowers average range.
Secondary factors
Cold and heat influence battery performance and thermal management load, changing consumption.
Headwind increases drag and consumption; tailwind can improve efficiency.
Rain and snow raise rolling resistance and often require additional climate/defrost usage.
Wheel size changes rotational losses and aerodynamic behavior, affecting total efficiency.
Incorrect tire pressure increases rolling resistance and can reduce range.
Battery degradation reduces usable energy capacity, shrinking achievable range.
Additional onboard consumers draw power from the traction battery and reduce driving distance.
Cabin comfort heating uses extra energy and is counted as an additional load.
Defrost and window heating improve visibility but consume additional battery power.
Audio and infotainment systems create a smaller but measurable constant energy draw.
